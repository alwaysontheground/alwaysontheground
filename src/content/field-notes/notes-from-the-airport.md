The flight from Amsterdam was delayed twice, and by the time I landed in Beirut it was past midnight. I'd booked nothing — no hotel, no driver. The plan was simple: walk out, find a taxi, head to the apartment in Achrafieh that a friend had arranged.

The arrivals hall was busier than I expected. Workers heading home from the Gulf, families waiting with bouquets, a small group holding a sign that read "Welcome home, jeddo."

I sat down for coffee. The barista, maybe twenty-five, asked where I was from. When I said the Netherlands he laughed. "Holland! My cousin lives in Eindhoven." The conversation lasted three minutes, ended in a phone number scribbled on a napkin, and gave me the first lead for a story I hadn't yet started writing.

This is what I keep relearning: the work doesn't begin when you reach the location. It begins the moment you stop trying to control where you are.
